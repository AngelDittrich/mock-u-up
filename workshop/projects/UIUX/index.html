Essay 01

Usability and Accessibility

Who can use it?

In modern software development, usability and accessibility are no longer optional features. They are core responsibilities of good design. Usability focuses on how easily a person can achieve a goal using a system, while accessibility ensures that this system can be used by as many people as possible. When usability is done well, users do not feel lost or frustrated; everything seems to work as expected.

A usable product guides the user without forcing them to think too much. Buttons behave consistently, actions produce clear feedback, and navigation feels predictable. The best usability is often invisible. Users do not stop to analyze the interface because it simply supports what they want to do. When friction appears, usability has failed.

A good example of usability can be seen in modern mobile operating systems. Over time, these systems have evolved from complex and crowded interfaces to more refined experiences. Gestures are consistent, animations follow the user’s actions, and transitions help explain what is happening. When a screen follows your finger during a swipe, the interaction feels natural and intuitive, reducing mental effort.

Accessibility adds another essential layer to design. A product can be usable for some users and still exclude others. Accessibility ensures that people with visual, motor, or cognitive limitations can also interact with the system. This includes readable text contrast, proper semantic structure for screen readers, and alternatives to precise mouse or touch input. These considerations are not special cases; they are part of responsible design.

Real-world applications often show both strengths and weaknesses in this area. Some platforms offer powerful features but present too much information at once, which can overwhelm users who struggle with attention or sensory overload. At the same time, strong keyboard navigation and shortcuts can greatly benefit users who cannot rely on a mouse. This contrast highlights an important challenge in design.

True usability and accessibility come from balance. Designers must offer powerful tools without sacrificing clarity, and flexibility without creating confusion. When both usability and accessibility are treated as priorities, products become not only more effective, but also more fair and inclusive for everyone who uses them.

Usability

Focuses on efficiency, clarity, and ease of use for the majority of users. A usable product reduces friction and cognitive load.

  • Clear navigation
  • Predictable behavior
  • Fast task completion

Accessibility

Ensures inclusion across physical, cognitive, and situational limitations. An accessible product removes barriers.

  • Screen reader support
  • Sufficient contrast
  • Multiple input methods
Essay 02

User Experience (UX)

How does it feel?

User Experience (UX) is often confused with User Interface (UI), but they are not the same thing. UI refers to what the user can see and interact with directly: buttons, colors, icons, and layouts. UX, on the other hand, is about how the user feels while using a product. If UI is the steering wheel and the dashboard, UX is the sensation of driving the car and how confident or comfortable you feel doing it.

My understanding of UX started to grow when I noticed that good design usually goes unnoticed. When a website or app works smoothly, users do not stop to think about why. They simply complete their task and move on. This is not accidental. UX is carefully planned to remove friction, confusion, and unnecessary effort. It focuses on making interactions feel natural, almost obvious.

UX is not limited to what happens on the screen. It includes the entire journey a user experiences: how they discover a product, how easy it is to learn, how problems are handled, and even how they feel when something goes wrong. A well-designed experience anticipates user needs and reduces frustration before it appears. This is why UX requires empathy as much as technical skill.

When using a checklist to evaluate UX, designers often rely on usability principles such as clarity, feedback, consistency, and familiarity with real-world concepts. Jakob Nielsen’s heuristics, like system visibility and error prevention, remain highly relevant today. They help ensure that a product is understandable and usable. However, modern UX goes one step further by focusing not only on function, but also on comfort and satisfaction.

This is where the emotional side of UX becomes important. Small details, such as smooth transitions, subtle animations, or clear confirmation messages, can make an experience feel polished and trustworthy. These micro-interactions may seem minor, but they show intention and care. They silently communicate to the user that the product was designed with their time and attention in mind.

In conclusion, the difference between a product that simply works and one that users enjoy often lies in UX. While UI makes things usable, UX makes them meaningful. As I continue learning about design, my goal is not just to create interfaces that function correctly, but experiences that feel respectful, intuitive, and human.
